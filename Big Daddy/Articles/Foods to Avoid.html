FOODS TO AVOID

Fancy eating liver with some fava beans and a nice chianti? Bad news - liver and alcohol are off the menu for (mother) for now.

Working out what foods (mother) can and can’t eat during pregnancy can be a minefield, with conflicting advice making it harder to follow than the Invisible Man. To save you the stress of frantic googling over a restaurant menu, we’ve created this handy list of foods for (mother) to enjoy and avoid in the UK:

Eat me:

  • runny chicken’s eggs, as long as they have a red lion stamp on their shell. The red lion shows that the eggs have been produced under a strict safety standard and are at very low risk of containing salmonella. So after years of doubt, most runny eggs now have the all-clear. Hurrah.
  • mayonnaise, mousse and soufflés, as long as they have been made with red lion-stamped eggs
  • all hard cheeses, even if they are unpasteurised. This includes cheddar, swiss cheese, parmesan and stilton.
  • pasteurised soft cheese. This includes mozzarella, cream cheese, ricotta, halloumi, paneer, cottage cheese, hard goat’s cheese and feta. Supermarket-bought cheeses are usually pasteurised - it’s the restaurant or farm shop stuff you have to watch out for.
  • shop bought yoghurt. If it’s homemade yoghurt, just make sure it’s been made with pasteurised milk.
  • ice cream. If it’s homemade, just be sure any eggs it contains have the red lion stamp.
  • peanuts
  • pepperoni, or any other cooked cured meats
  • pre-packed meat (e.g. ham)
  • tuna, but no more than two steaks or four tins per week as it contains mercury
  • oily fish (such as fresh tuna, mackerel, salmon, trout and herring) but no more than two portions per week as it can contain some harmful pollutants
  • white fish
  • shellfish
  • smoked fish (including smoked salmon and smoked trout)
  • raw wild fish (such as in sushi), as long as it has been previously frozen or if you freeze it for at least four days before using it. Freezing kills any parasitic worms it might contain.
  • raw farmed fish, even if it hasn’t been previously frozen
  • caffeine, but no more than 200mg per day. This is the equivalent of two mugs of instant coffee or tea, or a large filter coffee. Cola and chocolate also contain caffeine, so watch out for this.

Avoid:

  • runny eggs without the red lion stamp. Do check when eating out whether they use red lion eggs; if they don’t, ask that the eggs be cooked thoroughly as there a risk of salmonella.
  • other types of runny eggs, such as duck eggs, goose eggs and quail eggs
  • soft cheese with white rinds. This means that brie, camembert and soft goat’s cheese are out - unless they have been cooked - as they could contain listeria. Bring on the baked camembert!
  • soft blue cheeses, unless cooked. This rules out gorgonzola, danish blue and roquefort.
  • unpasteurised milk
  • pâté (including vegetable pâté), due to the risk of listeria
  • raw or undercooked meat, due to the risk of toxoplasmosis
  • cured meats, again due to the risk of toxoplasmosis, unless they are cooked or you have frozen the meat at home for four days
  • liver (or foods containing liver, such as pâté or haggis) due to its high concentration of vitamin A
  • vitamin A supplements
  • game which has been shot with lead pellets
  • shark, swordfish or marlin
  • raw shellfish
  • liquorice root (although liquorice sweets and teas are fine)
  • Venezuelan beaver cheese (no formal guidance available)

If you’re eating outside the UK, don’t forget to check the recommendations for pregnant women in that country.

It’s best not to stuff your face with a brie and swordfish sandwich when (mother) is looking enviously on. As a general rule, have what she’s having.
