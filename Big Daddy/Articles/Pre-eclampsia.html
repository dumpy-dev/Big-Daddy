PRE-ECLAMPSIA

Pre-eclampsia is a medical condition which causes high blood pressure. It affects around 6% of pregnant women. In most cases, it is quite mild. However, in around 1-2% of pregnancies, severe pre-eclampsia (which is a serious health risk to the mother and baby) can develop.

Pre-eclampsia is usually picked up during a routine antenatal appointment, typically after 24-26 weeks, where the mother has:

  • high blood pressure; and
  • protein in her urine (proteinuria)

Lots of women have either high blood pressure or proteinuria, so it usually only indicates pre-eclampsia if both of these are found at the same time.

As the pregnancy progresses, some women with pre-eclampsia may develop other symptoms, including:

  • severe headaches
  • swollen face, ankles, feet or hands
  • blurred vision or (seeing) flashing lights
  • severe heartburn
  • nausea or vomiting
  • pain below the ribs

If (mother) has any of the above symptoms, she should call her GP straight away, even if she hasn’t had a pre-eclampsia diagnosis.

Pre-eclampsia can cause the baby to grow more slowly than expected, as it’s not getting a good enough blood supply through the placenta. 

Where untreated, pre-eclampsia can be very dangerous. In rare cases, it can lead to eclampsia (fitting or convulsions), something called HELLP syndrome or even a stroke.

Unfortunately, there is no cure for pre-eclampsia apart from delivering the baby. Women with pre-eclampsia are usually admitted to hospital for constant monitoring until the baby can be delivered (usually via induction or caesarian section) around 37 weeks. If the mum’s symptoms get worse before 37 weeks, the baby may need to be delivered early. 

Once the baby is born, the mum’s pre-eclampsia usually gets better. However, both mum and baby usually need to stay in hospital for a few days after delivery for monitoring, and the mum will need to take medication to lower her blood pressure for a few weeks.

Nobody really knows what causes pre-eclampsia, but 1 in 6 women who have had pre-eclampsia will develop it again in a future pregnancy. If (mother) is diagnosed with pre-eclampsia, try not to worry. She - and (the baby) - will be monitored closely and will be in safe hands.
